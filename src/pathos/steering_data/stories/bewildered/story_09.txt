The suitcase handle bit into the parent’s palm as they watched the youngest child vanish around the corner of the university gate, the last solid shape dissolving into the morning sun. Their own breath hitched, a sudden tightness in the chest making the world tilt slightly. *Why did I let this happen?* The thought echoed, sharp and unfamiliar, as the child’s backpack strap swung free. The parent’s fingers, usually steady, trembled over the suitcase’s edge, the cool metal a strange anchor against the swirling confusion. 

Later, standing alone on the empty street, the parent traced the faded paint on the old mailbox with a knuckle that felt suddenly too heavy. The car’s engine had died a mile back, leaving only the echo of the child’s voice on the wind—a sound that felt both distant and impossibly close. They realized, with a quiet shock, that the person who had been watching the world shrink was now the one who had to grow into it, the weight of the empty space between them and the child’s new life pressing in like a stone.
