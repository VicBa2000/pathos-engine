The sun hung low, painting the driveway in long, cool shadows. I hadn’t moved since the alarm had screamed—my neck stiff from ignoring it, my eyelids heavy as wet stones. The rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of the tow truck’s jack began near the gate, a sound that felt like the world itself was stretching slowly, reluctantly. My foot remained planted on the cold floor of the garage, the familiar weight of the car’s absence a hollow ache behind my ribs. I didn’t lift my head. The smell of damp concrete and distant diesel mixed with the silence of my own stillness. *Just let it go,* the thought drifted, thick and slow as syrup. *Let it go.*  

Outside, the tow truck’s lights blinked like tired eyes. I watched the gate swing shut with a soft sigh, then lay back against the garage door, the weight of the empty space pressing down. My breath came in slow, shallow gulps. The driveway stretched ahead, empty and silent, and I didn’t feel the need to fill it. The world moved on, and I stayed exactly where I was—no more, no less.
