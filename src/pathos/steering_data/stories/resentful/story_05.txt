The contractor’s jackhammer began its low, insistent thud against the old brick foundation, a sound that vibrated up through the soles of my worn sneakers. My chest tightened, a familiar pressure building behind my sternum, making it hard to draw a full breath. *Another week*, I thought bitterly, watching the neighbor’s freshly painted door swing open to reveal a stack of plywood stacked neatly against the porch railing. "Sawdust in the air," my neighbor called out, his voice too bright for the hour, "just getting the framing right." I didn’t reply, my jaw clenching as the rhythmic pounding echoed the frantic rhythm of my own pulse in my temples. The dryness in my mouth was sharp, the smell of sawdust and wet concrete mixing with the lingering scent of last night’s rain on my own skin. My fingers, resting on the cool wooden railing, felt suddenly heavy, the wood grain rough against my calloused thumb. This wasn’t just noise; it was the slow, deliberate erasure of the space between us, brick by brick, beam by beam. The hammer’s rhythmic rhythm felt like a countdown to something I couldn’t name, but knew with cold certainty was already ending my peace.
