My knuckles were white on the hospital chair’s edge, the vinyl cold against my skin. The room smelled of antiseptic and old tears. I cleared my throat, the sound too loud in the silence, and began the first sentence of the story. My voice felt thick, like swallowing gravel. Across the table, Mrs. Chen’s eyes were closed, her breathing shallow. I noticed the way her thin hand trembled slightly on the blanket. The words came out flat, unconvincing. I wanted to shove the book away, to leave this place immediately. But then her eyelids flickered open, just a fraction, and her lips moved silently—like a fish gasping in shallow water. I held the book tighter, the pages rough against my palms. My chest tightened, not with compassion, but with the sudden, sharp weight of something heavy and wrong.
