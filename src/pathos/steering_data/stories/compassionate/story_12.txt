My eyes lingered on the neighbor’s hands as they carefully set the first brick, knuckles white against the damp wood. The morning air smelled of wet earth and fresh paint, a sharp contrast to the quiet tension in their shoulders. My throat tightened slightly, a familiar ache spreading from my chest. Their voice, barely audible over the distant sound of the city, was strained: "It’s so hard, just getting this right..." I watched their steady breathing falter for a moment before they resumed, the small tremor in their hand a silent plea. 

Later, when the dust settled and the rhythmic hammering had softened to a hush, I found myself stepping closer to the ladder they’d left leaning against the wall. My fingers brushed the cold metal rung, the rough texture a grounding counterpoint to the quiet relief I felt. The neighbor’s head turned, their eyes holding a fragile hope. "You’re here," they murmured, their voice thick. Without thinking, I reached for the ladder, my hand steady now, the shared silence between us warm enough to hold the weight of their effort.
