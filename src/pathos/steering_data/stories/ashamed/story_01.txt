The cracked concrete path under his worn boots felt like a betrayal. He stopped where the skeletal remains of the old oak stood, its roots twisted in the rubble of what had been his childhood home. A cold stone settled in his chest, heavier than the dust on the broken brick wall. He could still smell the dry earth and lemon cleaner from his grandmother’s kitchen, but now it was mixed with the acrid tang of fresh concrete and the distant, angry drone of machinery. His throat tightened, a tightness that made breathing shallow, as he remembered the last time he’d stood here, eight years ago, promising his son he’d be back. Now the promise felt like a ghost.
