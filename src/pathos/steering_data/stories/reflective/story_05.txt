The hammer’s rhythm matched the slow beat of my own pulse as I watched Mr. Henderson plant the third post. His calloused hands, dusting the concrete with each strike, were steady, purposeful. I felt the weight of the empty chair beside me pressing against my ribs, a sensation that had been there since the last storm washed away the garden path. His young daughter laughed from the back porch, a sound that felt suddenly too bright, too sharp against the quiet afternoon. "Just for the dogs," he said, his voice low, not looking up from the post he’d just driven. The words settled in my throat like a stone, heavier than the hammer’s weight.
