The antiseptic smell clung to her clothes, sharp and cold. Her hands, usually steady, trembled slightly as she adjusted the oxygen mask over the patient’s thin face. She knelt beside the bed, the thin plastic sheet of the patient’s gown catching the fluorescent light. This man with his quiet eyes and the way his fingers twisted the edge of the blanket – it was the same quiet desperation she remembered in her brother’s eyes when he was sick, just before the hospital bed became his final resting place. She felt the familiar ache in her chest, a slow tide pulling her deeper into a memory she couldn’t surface. His hand, small and cool against hers, felt like the hand her brother had once held when they were children, searching for the right word.

The patient’s voice was a whisper, barely audible over the hum of the machines. "Do you remember?" he asked, his eyes fixed on her face, a question that landed like a stone in still water. She froze, the oxygen mask suddenly heavy on her face, the trembling stopped for a second before it began again, a smaller vibration in her wrist.
