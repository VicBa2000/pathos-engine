The cardboard box lay open on the worn velvet armchair, its contents spilling out like a quiet river of yellowed paper. I traced my fingertip over the neat, slightly smudged handwriting on a fourth-grade report card – *'Good effort, needs more practice'* – the familiar, gentle weight of it settling in my chest. My shoulders dropped, the tightness from the day’s rush finally dissolving into a deep, slow warmth that started in my hands and spread through my bones. *She always knew*, I thought, the memory of my mother’s quiet voice from the kitchen, the scent of lavender soap lingering in the air, more vivid than any school report. The box felt heavy with care, not burden.

Mother’s hands, resting on the arm of the chair, were steady as she pulled a single, perfect blue pencil from her pocket. She didn’t look up, just watched the dust motes dance in the sunbeam, her breathing slow and deep. The quiet house felt large and safe, the sound of my own breath matching the rhythm of her stillness. I didn’t need to say anything; the warmth in my chest was enough. It was a comfort that felt like coming home.
