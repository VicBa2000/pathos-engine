My knuckles were white on the railing as I watched Mr. Henderson’s crew hammer the first fence post into the dirt. The smell of fresh concrete and wet earth hung heavy in the afternoon air. He’d been measuring the property line with a tape measure for hours, his brow furrowed like a mountain range, his voice low and insistent when he finally spoke to me: "This is for the kids, you know." His hands, rough and stained, moved with a confidence that felt like an accusation. I stood too close, the heat from the sun suddenly tight in my chest, my throat closing as I swallowed hard, the taste of metal sharp in my mouth. 

Later, as the new fence rose higher, its posts bleeding rust into the soil, I felt my pulse hammering in my temples. My jaw was locked tight, a cold stone in my stomach.
