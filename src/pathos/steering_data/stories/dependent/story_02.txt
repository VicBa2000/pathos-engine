The niece’s pencil hovered over the paper, her small hand steady despite the tremor in her wrist. Her drawing showed a small, round face with large, knowing eyes, wearing a hat that looked suspiciously like the one her grandfather wore during summer storms. Uncle Ben’s throat tightened, a sudden, cold pressure behind his eyes. He knew that face better than his own reflection in the window glass—its softness, the way it watched the world with a quiet understanding he’d never fully shared. He felt the couch cushion sink beneath his fingers as he shifted, the familiar ache in his chest deepening, not with sadness, but with the heavy, quiet certainty that this small figure, this child’s invention, had always been his anchor, his only true compass. He couldn’t speak, couldn’t move, only watched as the niece lifted her chin, her gaze meeting his, asking where it came from. The drawing was too real, the eyes too knowing, and he knew the truth: he had carried this small friend inside him for so long, a quiet weight he’d never named.
