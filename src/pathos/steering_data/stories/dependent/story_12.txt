The coach’s words hung in the air like dust motes after the final whistle, heavy and final. His fingers, usually steady, trembled as he pushed the rejection letter across the table. The athlete watched his own reflection in the dark window—eyes wide, jaw tight—his breath shallow and quick. The team was a lifeline, a place where his body knew the rhythm of effort, where every step felt purposeful. Now, the silence pressed in, louder than the gym’s echo, and his hands clenched into fists that felt useless.

I had to breathe. The letter felt heavy in my pocket, a stone against my thigh. Outside, the city lights blurred, but inside, everything was sharp and sudden. My coach’s quiet reply—*You'll be back*—sounded like a promise written in smoke. I couldn’t remember the last time I’d felt truly whole, not just strong. My chest ached with a pressure that wasn’t from exertion, but from the space left when the only place I belonged had slipped away. I had to breathe.
