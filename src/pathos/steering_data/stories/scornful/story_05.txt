The cold metal of the classroom door handle bit into my palm as I stood there, the bell’s final chime echoing in the empty hall. My daughter’s small hand, warm and sticky with sweat, squeezed mine. "Dad, Mrs. Henderson’s the teacher," she said, her voice bright, oblivious. The name landed like a stone in my stomach. *Mrs. Henderson*. The name that felt like a fist in my throat from the tenth grade, the one who’d made me feel invisible in the halls, the one whose sharp critiques had been the only constant in my childhood. My fingers went white on the doorframe, the wood cold against my skin. I watched her face, so young, so utterly unburdened by the memory I carried like a stone in my chest. She’d never known that ache, that deep, silent bitterness that had been my constant companion for years. Her bright eyes, fixed on the teacher’s approaching form, were worlds away from the quiet storm I’d been.

Mrs. Henderson’s smile was gentle as she reached for the child’s hand.
