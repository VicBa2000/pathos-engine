The dust motes danced in the afternoon sun slanting through the half-open window of the old bedroom, a familiar scent of mildew and forgotten clothes clashing with the sterile smell of the new bed. Their own reflection in the cracked mirror showed someone who’d just arrived, shoulders unnaturally stiff, the tightness in their throat a familiar sour taste. Outside, their father’s voice was low, almost a rumble, as he shifted in his chair at the kitchen table, his hands resting on the worn wood, knuckles white where they gripped the edge. "You’re home," he said, not looking up, the words hanging heavy in the silence that felt like a physical weight pressing down on their chest. They hadn’t meant to be back here, not like this, not after all the years spent building a life elsewhere, but the door to their old room stood open, a silent invitation that now felt like a trap.
