My hands felt cold, the metal of the park bench biting through my thin jacket. Lena’s eyes widened as she stared at the empty swing set, her voice tight. "That night? You said we had the picnic under the old oak." I blinked, the memory of that sticky grass, the laughter, the sudden rain – it was all gone now, replaced by this strange, hollow space where it should have been. "We didn’t," I said, the words dry as dust. "We were running home from the library. Rain started, and I got caught in the alley." Her face crumpled, the confusion sharp in her eyes. "But you told me we had the picnic," she murmured, the words barely audible. I could see the frustration in her jaw, the way her shoulders stiffened as she looked away. The air felt stale, thick with the weight of two different stories. My throat tightened, not with emotion I could name, but with the sudden, sharp ache of understanding. She turned back, knuckles white on the bench, her gaze fixed on the rain-slicked pavement where our shared past should have been. I didn’t move. The silence between us wasn’t empty – it was full of the chasm where one memory ended and the other began.
