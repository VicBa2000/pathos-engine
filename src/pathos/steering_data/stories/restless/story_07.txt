The dim light of the studio window caught the dust motes dancing above the desk where the manuscript lay open, its pages already stained with coffee rings. Across the room, the writer paced, bare feet scraping the worn floorboards, each step a measured drumbeat against the silence of repeated no. His hands, usually steady with ink, felt cold and slick against the edge of the table. *Publisher A said the story was "too niche,"* he’d whispered to his friend last week, the words echoing in the quiet apartment. *Publisher B called it "unoriginal,"* he’d muttered again, the taste of stale coffee sharp in his mouth. *Publisher C…* the thought hung there, heavier than the rejection letter he hadn’t opened since the third one came. He stared at the ceiling, the pattern of cracks like a map of failure, his knuckles white where they gripped the edge of his chair. His hand trembled slightly as he reached for the phone, the silence pressing in so thick it felt like the walls were breathing.
