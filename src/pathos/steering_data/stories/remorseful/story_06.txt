The dust kicked up from the neighbor’s freshly tamped gravel path, stinging the eyes of the silent man who stood watching. His throat felt dry, a familiar tightness in his chest that had nothing to do with the late afternoon sun. He’d been too quick to judge that neighbor years ago, his sharp words echoing now with the sound of the sledgehammer hitting wood. *I never meant to be so hard*, the neighbor had said the other day, voice low, almost too quiet to hear over the city’s hum, *but I felt it*. Now, seeing the scaffolding rise against the old clapboard house, the familiar ache flared—a low vibration in his bones, a sudden urge to sink into the ground. He traced the rough edge of his worn jacket, fingers trembling slightly, the dust clinging to his skin like a second skin.
