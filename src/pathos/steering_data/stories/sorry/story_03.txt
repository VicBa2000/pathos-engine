The cardboard box sat open on the kitchen floor, a silent accusation in the quiet house. My fingers traced the brittle edge of the first math paper, its corners crumbling under the pressure. The scent of old paper and dust hit me like a cold hand. *All* the assignments, neatly labeled, tucked away in this one box since they were first collected. My stomach clenched, a tight stone settling in my chest. I remembered my mother’s quiet smile when I’d mentioned forgetting a project, the way she’d just say, "It’s fine, honey," but now the silence of that box held a different weight—thick, heavy, impossible to ignore. My throat tightened, dry and constricted, as I thought of her hands, steady and careful, holding all these pages for me while I’d been too young to understand the value of keeping them. The light from the window caught the stacked papers, turning them pale, and for a moment, I felt the sharp sting of something unspoken, the quiet ache of everything she’d carried for me without saying a word.
