The license slipped into his hand, cold and crisp, but his fingers trembled so badly he nearly dropped it. Outside the testing center window, the afternoon sun glared off wet asphalt, a harsh contrast to the slow, heavy thud of his own heartbeat in his chest. The officer’s smile felt like a thin, temporary paper cut. “You’re good,” he’d said, the words tasting like ash. Inside, the silence after that was a physical weight, pressing down on his ribs. He’d passed the test, the car was waiting, but the key felt heavier than stone in his pocket.  

He walked home, the familiar streetlights casting long, pale shadows. His shoulders sagged, each step a struggle against the sudden, deep quiet that settled in his throat. The car’s headlights would have been a lifeline, but now the road ahead seemed endless, smooth and unmarked. He didn’t want to drive.
