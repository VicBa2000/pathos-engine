My fingers traced the edge of the cardboard box, the brittle sound of old paper scraping against my knuckles. The stack of assignments—seventy-three years of mine—felt heavier than the books I’d carried through school, each page a silent testament to effort. My chest tightened with warmth, a deep, unfamiliar pressure that had nothing to do with the cold room. *She kept them all*, I thought, the realization hitting like a stone settling. The smell of faded ink and time rose in my throat. 

"Always kept them," Mom murmured, her hand brushing mine where it rested on the box, her thumb tracing the spine of a math textbook. Her eyes held the quiet strength of someone who’d watched me stumble, yet never let go.
