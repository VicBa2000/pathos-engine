My fingers trembled slightly as I lifted the worn book, the scent of old paper sharp in the quiet room. Outside the window, late afternoon sun painted the hallway gold, catching the dust motes dancing in the air. The first patient, Mrs. Chen, lay back in her bed, her breathing shallow but steady. "This is the first time," she whispered, her voice thin as tissue paper. I cleared my throat, beginning the story softly. As the words formed a rhythm against the silence, her eyes, usually shadowed, seemed to lift. A slow, almost imperceptible warmth spread through her chest, her breath deepening with each sentence. The light on her face changed—less pale, more alive, as if the story had found its way into the quiet space where her thoughts lived. Her hand, resting on the bedsheet, curled slightly, a tiny gesture that spoke of something new taking root. I watched, the tremor in my hands fading as the quiet room filled with the shared breath of two people, the story thrumming between them like a steady pulse.
