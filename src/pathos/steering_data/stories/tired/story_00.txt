The neighbor’s dog, a frantic blur of brown and white, tore through the overgrown tomato patch, claws sinking deep into the earth. Soil clumped under its frantic paws, lifting a neat row of seedlings like tiny, uprooted ghosts. Sarah’s hands, already scraped from yesterday’s garden work, felt heavy as stones. She watched the dog’s relentless digging, the way it seemed to chase shadows beneath the soil, a creature driven by a hunger that had nothing to do with food. Her eyelids felt thick, watered, refusing to stay open. The sun beat down, making the dusty air taste like old pennies. She wanted to sit, just sit, but the thought of the dog’s next charge made her knees feel suddenly weak. "Stop," she whispered, the words tasting dry, barely audible over the crunch of earth.
